# Facilitator Guide

This is not a lecture about a finished mechanism. You bring the room an open
problem and let them reproduce the corrections that shaped the research.

The session succeeds when trainees can say:

> “The contribution is already recorded. The mechanism that uses it is a bet.
> I can name what ‘best’ means, what would kill my candidate, and what evidence
> would change my mind.”

## Workshop Contract

### Fixed premise

The DAO counts calls, study, mentoring, maintenance, and similar activity as
contributions. Do not let the room spend the workshop relitigating this premise.

### Open question

How should prior contribution records interact with a later witnessed result?

### What trainees may produce

- candidate mechanism sketches;
- research prize cards;
- open bet cards;
- attack findings;
- proposed prior-art lanes;
- witness designs;
- mock adjudication records;
- typed negatives and residue.

### What trainees may not produce through this workshop

- an implemented mechanism;
- a payout, token, reputation, or governance rule;
- a claim that one mechanism is validated;
- a change to the repository registers;
- authority to promote their exercise output.

## Room Setup

- **Duration:** 90 minutes.
- **Group size:** 6–30 people; use groups of 3–5.
- **Materials:** projected presentation, one workbook per person or group,
  markers or a shared document, and a visible timer.
- **Roles:** `proposer`, `skeptic`, `witness designer`, and `boundary auditor`.
  With groups of three, combine witness designer and boundary auditor.
- **Facilitator posture:** ask for a concrete record before offering formal
  vocabulary. Do not rescue an attractive mechanism from its own falsifier.

## Run of Show

| Time | Phase | Trainee output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0–8 min | opening and fixed premise | shared understanding of what is fixed and open |
| 8–18 min | case and mechanism generation | at least three candidate mechanisms |
| 18–28 min | define “best” | frozen comparison criteria |
| 28–42 min | mechanism-as-bet | one prize card and one bet card per group |
| 42–53 min | attack exchange | collapse-test and attack findings |
| 53–64 min | split hidden promises | recognition, community, and communication bets |
| 64–70 min | two-language check | narrative/operational mapping |
| 70–76 min | directed subtower prior art | bet-linked questions, bounds, negative precedents, and exclusions |
| 76–84 min | witness design | one preregistered witness card revised by prior art |
| 84–88 min | mock adjudication | open/survived/falsified/invalid decision with limits |
| 88–90 min | close | one sentence the trainee can transfer to another mechanism |

## Moment 1 — Fix the premise

**Projected copy**

> The contribution already counts.
>
> What should happen next?

**Spoken copy**

Use the approved opening from
[APPROVED-LANGUAGE-AUDITION.md](APPROVED-LANGUAGE-AUDITION.md).

Then add: “Put a box around that premise in your workbook. We are not deciding
whether calls count. We are deciding what a later mechanism may do with those
contribution records.”

**Interaction prompt**

Ask each group to write one sentence beginning:

> “After a contribution is recorded, the DAO could…”

**Facilitation note**

If a group says “decide whether it was a contribution,” point to the fixed
premise and ask them to move one step later in the lifecycle.

## Moment 2 — Establish the workshop posture

**Projected copy**

> Let’s try to break an idea.

**Spoken copy**

“I’m not going to teach you the correct mechanism today. I’m going to bring you
an idea, and together we’re going to make it precise enough that it can fail.
That is how this research harness protects us from falling in love with our own
design.”

**Interaction prompt**

Ask: “When you hear ‘break an idea,’ what would count as a useful failure?”

Take two answers. Do not define `typed negative` yet.

**Facilitation note**

Reward concrete answers such as “we discover the wrong people receive credit”
or “review becomes too expensive.” Redirect answers about presentation quality
back to mechanism behavior.

## Moment 3 — Introduce the case

**Projected copy**

> 20 governance calls
>
> → one major governance proposal

**Spoken copy**

“Imagine I attended twenty governance calls. Months later, I release a major
governance proposal that the DAO can witness. The calls are contribution
records. The proposal is a witnessed result. What mechanism, if any, should
connect the two?”

**Interaction prompt**

Groups have four minutes to produce at least three mechanisms. They must include
one mechanism they dislike.

**Facilitation note**

Do not project candidate answers yet. If the room stalls, ask only:

- “Could everything accumulate?”
- “Could the subject matter limit relevance?”
- “Could review begin only after a result exists?”

These are prompts, not recommendations.

## Moment 4 — Compare the mechanism space

After groups report, cluster their proposals. Only then reveal the three common
families.

**Projected copy**

> Accumulate
>
> Bound by subject
>
> Review after a result

**Spoken copy**

“These are not the only possibilities. They are three useful contrasts. One
stores contribution value over time. One allows only related subjects to
connect. One waits for a witnessed result and reviews the earlier records then.
None is our answer yet.”

**Interaction prompt**

Ask: “What could each mechanism accidentally train people to optimize?”

**Facilitation note**

Keep separate columns for desired behavior and dangerous optimization. Make
sure attendance farming, topic-label gaming, hindsight narratives, reviewer
capture, and exclusion can appear without treating them as predetermined facts.

## Moment 5 — Define “best” before choosing

**Projected copy**

> Best according to what?

**Spoken copy**

“If we say we want the best mechanism, we need to say what best means before we
see which option wins. Otherwise we can change the definition until our favorite
mechanism looks good.”

**Interaction prompt**

Give groups three minutes to choose:

- one primary criterion;
- three blocking guardrails;
- one cost they are willing to accept;
- one cost they are not willing to accept.

Then reveal the default comparison set:

1. result-linked recognition usefulness;
2. false-credit acceptance;
3. contribution-farming resistance;
4. review burden;
5. access and inclusion;
6. entitlement leakage;
7. authority separation.

**Facilitation note**

Do not let a weighted total hide a blocking failure. A candidate that performs
well overall but creates automatic authority does not “win on average.”

## Moment 6 — Reveal the core correction

**Projected copy**

> The contribution is known.
>
> The mechanism is the bet.

**Spoken copy**

Use the approved reveal. Then say:

“Write your mechanism as a prediction, not a feature. What will become better
than the baseline? Under what conditions? What one fact would make you drop it?”

Only now introduce the terms:

- **Prize:** the bounded research target.
- **Bet:** the falsifiable mechanism conjecture.
- **Witness:** the evidence required to adjudicate it.
- **Collapse-test:** the fact or failure that zeros the candidate’s contribution.

**Interaction prompt**

Groups complete Workbook Rounds 3 and 4: one prize card and one mechanism bet.

## Moment 7 — Attack someone else’s favorite

**Projected copy**

> Swap. Attack. Try to kill it.

**Spoken copy**

“Pass your bet to another group. Your job is not to improve their wording. Find
the easiest strategy for gaming it, the missing comparison, or the consequence
they forgot to measure.”

**Interaction prompt**

The attacking group must return:

- one concrete exploit or failure path;
- one missing witness;
- one condition that would make the bet vacuous;
- one part that remains worth testing even if the bet dies.

**Facilitation note**

When groups receive attacks, do not let them defend immediately. First require
them to restate the attack in a form the attacking group accepts.

## Moment 8 — Split the promises

**Projected copy**

> One mechanism. Three bets.

**Spoken copy**

“Even if your mechanism improves recognition, that does not show it helps the
community. Even if people like it, that does not show the attribution is right.
And even if they understand the metaphor, that does not show either of the
others. These are three separate bets.”

**Interaction prompt**

Groups write one conjecture and one witness for each:

- recognition utility;
- community utility;
- communication safety.

Then ask them to draw arrows only for evidence order, never evidence inheritance.

**Facilitation note**

Use the registered
[Deferred Contribution prize family](../PRIZE-REGISTER.md#deferred-contribution-model-prize-family)
as the source example. Do not imply that its open bets survived.

## Moment 9 — Keep the human language

**Projected copy**

> Two languages. One authority.

**Spoken copy**

“We nearly removed words like investment, realization, and cashing because they
can imply entitlement. But then the mechanism became difficult to explain. The
repair was not to choose one language. It was to keep the narrative language
for people and bind it to one operational meaning.”

**Interaction prompt**

Groups complete the bilingual table:

| Narrative term | Operational record or event | Consequence it must not imply |
| --- | --- | --- |
| investment |  |  |
| realization |  |  |
| cashing |  |  |

**Facilitation note**

If a group writes “payout” as the operational meaning of cashing, ask what
evidence or owner authorized that transition. The expected answer in this
research exercise is: none.

## Moment 10 — Route the bet through relevant subtowers

**Projected copy**

> Send the bet into the relevant subtowers.

**Spoken copy**

“Now we research outside our own idea. We do not ask every subtower a generic
question, and we do not collect citations to make the mechanism look credible.
For every subtower that can change this bet, write one bounded question tied to
the bet or its collapse-test. Require a precedent that attacks the mechanism,
name the bridge risk, and end with the next thing we would try to falsify.”

**Interaction prompt**

Groups complete Workbook Round 8. They must:

- select every subtower with a non-vacuous question that could change the bet,
  collapse-test, or witness;
- tie each selected subtower to one of the three independent bets;
- bound the allowed source kind, depth, and quota;
- require at least one negative precedent or record that the bounded search
  found none;
- state the local bridge risk or disanalogy and the next falsifier;
- record an exclusion reason for every subtower not selected.

Use the shared
[mechanism-directed prior-art contract](../../subtowers/MECHANISM-PRIOR-ART-CONTRACT.md)
as the return shape.

**Facilitation note**

Prior art can revise a bet or improve its witness. It cannot return `SURVIVED`.
Do not let groups treat source count, repeated warnings, or a formal model as
authority. If they cannot say what decision a subtower could change, that
subtower is excluded for this bounded pass and the exclusion stays as residue.

## Moment 11 — Design evidence that can disagree with you

**Projected copy**

> What evidence would change your mind?

**Spoken copy**

“A good witness is not a demonstration where your mechanism gets to look good.
It is a comparison that can return an answer you do not like.”

**Interaction prompt**

Groups complete the witness card:

- unit and eligible population;
- mechanism condition and baseline;
- primary outcome;
- blocking guardrails;
- frozen thresholds;
- exclusions and missing-data rule;
- `SURVIVED`, `FALSIFIED`, and `INVALID` conditions.

**Facilitation note**

Fixtures may prove that the record shape and calculations work. They do not show
that the mechanism improves recognition or community outcomes. Require groups to
name which evidence layer they are designing.

## Moment 12 — Practice a bounded verdict

**Projected copy**

> Open  ·  Survived  ·  Falsified  ·  Invalid

**Spoken copy**

“Most real bets in this repository are still open. That is not failure. It means
the required witness has not run. A survived verdict is also narrow: it means
the frozen condition passed under the tested scope, not that the DAO should
deploy the mechanism.”

**Interaction prompt**

Give each group one mock result from the facilitator’s choice:

- attribution improves, but review time triples;
- recognition accuracy holds, but community participation becomes more
  performative;
- comprehension improves, but guaranteed-return expectations also rise;
- required data is missing for half the cases.

They must choose a verdict, explain its scope, and preserve a typed negative.

## Moment 13 — Close with transfer

**Projected copy**

> Leave with a better question, not a prettier answer.

**Spoken copy**

“The point of this harness is not to make every idea survive. It is to stop an
idea from becoming attractive fiction. The contribution can be real while the
mechanism is wrong. Your job is to keep those two facts separate.”

**Interaction prompt**

Each participant completes this sentence aloud or in writing:

> “The next time I see an attractive mechanism, I will ask…”

## Debrief Questions

Ask no more than three, depending on time:

1. Which criterion changed your preferred mechanism?
2. Which attack created the most useful revision or typed negative?
3. Where did you accidentally treat a contribution record as entitlement?
4. Which of the three independent bets was easiest to neglect?
5. Which subtower question changed the bet or witness, and which one was merely
   decorative?
6. What evidence layer did you initially overestimate?

## After the Room

Record:

- where participants misunderstood the fixed premise;
- whether they could distinguish mechanism candidates from bets;
- whether they froze criteria before choosing;
- whether they separated the three bets;
- whether they selected subtowers by a bet-linked question rather than by
  mechanical fan-out;
- whether each selected subtower required a negative precedent and bridge risk;
- whether fixture evidence was confused with mechanism evidence;
- whether anyone inferred payout or authority;
- which slide or spoken explanation caused the confusion.

Use [VALIDATION.md](VALIDATION.md) for the first cohort trial. Do not silently
rewrite this guide after a failed moment; append the correction and then revise.

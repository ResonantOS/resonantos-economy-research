### Formal-Mechanisms Subtower Source Cards

1. **van Seijen et al., “True Online Temporal-Difference Learning” (JMLR 2016).** [Citation](https://www.jmlr.org/papers/v17/15-599.html)
   **Kind:** related evidence / primary technical paper. **Supported:** true-online TD(λ) maintains exact online equivalence to the forward view; eligibility traces provide computationally cheap temporally decayed propagation. **Assumptions:** Markov reward process or MDP, chosen policy, value-learning objective, selected γ/λ and representation. **Unsupported inference:** elapsed time or trace weight measures human contribution, causality, merit, or entitlement. **Bridge risk:** severe proximity bias—recent visible activity receives weight even when irrelevant, while latent preparation may be absent from state. **Owner:** TD(λ)/true-online TD family, van Seijen–Sutton et al. **Posture:** enabling computation; warning socially. **Fixture implication:** delayed chain with one relevant early action and one irrelevant late action; replay `e_t=(γλ)e_{t-1}+x_t`, compare online and forward-view attribution. **Next falsifier:** attribution changes materially under harmless event insertion or plausible λ choices.

2. **Arjona-Medina et al., “RUDDER: Return Decomposition for Delayed Rewards” (NeurIPS 2019).** [Citation](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper/2019/hash/16105fb9cc614fc29e1bda00dab60d41-Abstract.html)
   **Kind:** related evidence / primary technical paper. **Supported:** for finite delayed-reward processes, return redistribution can preserve total return and optimal policies while moving expected reward toward contributing time steps; optimal redistribution may require a non-Markov sequence process. **Assumptions:** specified scalar return, finite horizon, learned return predictor, policy-relative decomposition. **Unsupported inference:** decomposed reward identifies socially deserving contributors or legitimate retrospective credit. **Bridge risk:** the outcome definition and training data silently decide what “contributed”; policy-relative attribution can rewrite history after preferences change. **Owner:** RUDDER/return-decomposition family. **Posture:** enabling with structural warning. **Fixture:** sequence with preparatory, irrelevant, and outcome-trigger events; require redistributed sum = observed outcome and zero contribution for a controlled irrelevant insertion. **Next falsifier:** equally predictive models assign incompatible credit while preserving return.

3. **Foerster et al., “Counterfactual Multi-Agent Policy Gradients” (AAAI 2018).** [Citation](https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/AAAI/article/view/11794)
   **Kind:** related evidence / primary technical paper. **Supported:** COMA computes an agent-specific advantage by marginalizing that agent’s action while holding other agents’ actions fixed, using a centralized critic. **Assumptions:** cooperative global reward, valid centralized Q-estimator, modeled action alternatives, stable agent boundary. **Unsupported inference:** the counterfactual is a real causal effect or fair human credit. **Bridge risk:** evaluator/model controls baselines; mentoring, maintenance, and coordination can be complementary and disappear under one-agent-at-a-time subtraction. **Owner:** COMA/counterfactual-baseline family. **Posture:** enabling marginal test; warning on baseline dependence. **Fixture:** two-person synergy plus redundant-action cases; vary only one action and report counterfactual delta with critic uncertainty. **Next falsifier:** rankings reverse under reasonable baselines or critic misspecification.

4. **Wang, Wiens & Lundberg, “Shapley Flow” (AISTATS 2021).** [Citation](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v130/wang21b.html)
   **Kind:** related evidence / primary technical paper. **Supported:** given a causal DAG, credit can be assigned to edges across the whole graph; flat node attribution can hide dependencies. **Assumptions:** acyclic causal graph, defensible graph boundary and structural dependencies, modeled intervention semantics. **Unsupported inference:** observed activity logs identify the true causal graph. **Bridge risk:** graph construction becomes the hidden adjudicator; omitted invisible work or a wrong edge manufactures relevance. **Owner:** Shapley Flow graph-attribution family. **Posture:** enabling relevance-path representation; warning on graph epistemics. **Fixture:** fork/mediator DAG for call→proposal→innovation; compare edge flows under two plausible graphs. **Next falsifier:** same observations support DAGs with contradictory contribution paths.

5. **Sundararajan, Dhamdhere & Agarwal, “Shapley-Taylor Interaction Index” (ICML 2020).** [Citation](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v119/sundararajan20a)
   **Kind:** related evidence / primary technical paper. **Supported:** attribution can be extended from individual features to interactions up to order `k`, under explicit linearity, dummy, symmetry, efficiency, and interaction-distribution axioms. **Assumptions:** defined coalition value function, chosen interaction order and baseline. **Unsupported inference:** these axioms encode community fairness or justify splitting interaction value among people. **Bridge risk:** scalar per-person totals erase joint work; high-order interactions become expensive and cutoff-dependent. **Owner:** Shapley-Taylor interaction family. **Posture:** enabling anti-collapse representation; warning against individual-only totals. **Fixture:** pure-pair AND/XOR contribution where neither activity suffices alone; require interaction mass remain explicit and conserved. **Next falsifier:** result changes sharply with cutoff `k` or coalition-value specification.

6. **Yang, Sun & Narasimhan, “A Generalized Algorithm for Multi-Objective RL and Policy Adaptation” (NeurIPS 2019).** [Citation](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper/2019/hash/4a46fbfca3f1465a27b210f4bdfe6ab3-Abstract.html)
   **Kind:** related evidence / primary technical paper. **Supported:** multiple competing objectives can be retained as vector returns and policies conditioned on explicit linear preferences; optimal behavior can change significantly when preferences change. **Assumptions:** measurable objective vector, linear preference family, RL setting. **Unsupported inference:** the objective axes or weights are socially legitimate. **Bridge risk:** a displayed scalar hides contestable trade-offs and makes chosen weights look factual. **Owner:** preference-conditioned MORL family. **Posture:** enabling vector preservation; warning on scalarization. **Fixture:** two evidence profiles—direct outcome help versus general capacity-building—whose ordering reverses under two declared weight vectors. **Next falsifier:** a universally dominant profile makes the vector distinction locally irrelevant.

### Aggregation alternatives

- Preserve an **edge-and-interaction record**: activity receipt, claimed outcome edge, temporal trace, direct/indirect path, interaction tuple, counterfactual delta, uncertainty. No automatic total.
- Keep a **vector evidence profile** and make any query-time preference explicit; do not store one canonical scalar.
- Use **graph attribution** only conditional on a contested DAG, with alternative-graph sensitivity.
- Keep **interaction mass separate** from individual mass.
- If caps, concavity, or decay are tested, treat them as post-attribution transforms and retain a residual bucket so mass does not silently vanish. Tractability establishes only computability.

**Supported claims**

Its strongest limiting sentence is: **“Attendance alone should not become a permanent substitute for contribution”**; useful call participation may eventually count.
- **§10 Governance:** the intended governance model is contextual and multi-factor, not one-token/one-vote.
- **§17.1 Roadmap:** test-phase rewards/reputation are intended to be honored or migrated only subject to technical verification, anti-abuse review, and final DAO-approved rules.
- **§21 Open Questions:** contribution-validation rules and anti-farming/anti-spam rules remain explicitly unresolved.
- **Investment framing boundary:** the preamble says the draft is **“not an investment prospectus”**; §2.2/FAQ denies traditional equity or legal-share framing; §15 distinguishes contribution income from passive capital income.

**Unsupported inference / residue**

The whitepaper does **not** specify a deferred-contribution mechanism that:

- records study, attendance, mentoring, or maintenance as merely preparatory evidence;
- later links that evidence to a witnessed result;
- establishes direct/adjacent/general-capacity relatedness;
- adjudicates counterfactual causality or contribution weight;
- aggregates receipts across time, people, or activities;
- distinguishes receipt, relevance claim, reviewed credit, reward eligibility, and authority;
- defines rejection, appeal, expiry, caps, decay, duplication, or retrospective anti-farming controls.

“Eventually count” does not establish retrospective causal attribution. Testnet migration does not establish delayed recognition of preparatory work. Whitepaper silence is residue, not support.

**Local bridge risk:** High. The draft itself sometimes places simple participation close to rewards and reputation, then places reputation close to governance influence and economic eligibility. Importing those statements as an operational rule could collapse attendance → credit → reward → authority, exactly where §21 admits validation and anti-farming rules remain open. “Investment” or “cashing” language would also conflict with the draft’s non-investment and non-equity boundaries.

**Ownership verdict:** The whitepaper owns the **high-level activity vocabulary and boundary intent**: calls, learning, mentoring, maintenance-like work, contribution recording, later eligibility, anti-attendance farming, and contextual governance. It owns **no operative part of the proposed deferred attribution mechanism**—no temporal linkage, relatedness rule, aggregation rule, or adjudication procedure.

**Next falsifier:** An official whitepaper revision would need to define a concrete prior-activity → later-result evidence chain, independent acceptance/rejection, temporal and relatedness rules, anti-farming limits, and explicit separation from automatic reward or authority. Until then, mechanism ownership remains absent.
